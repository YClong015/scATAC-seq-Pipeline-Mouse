## Abstract

Age-related diseases of distinct organ systems — chronic kidney disease, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and aortic aneurysm and dissection — share a common burden of cellular stress, inflammation, and progressive loss of tissue function. Whether these convergent phenotypes are mirrored by a convergent transcription factor (TF) programme at the level of chromatin accessibility remains poorly defined. Here, we integrated four single-cell ATAC-seq (scATAC-seq) datasets covering mouse kidney ischemia-reperfusion injury (Muto et al., 2024), cigarette-smoke- and lipopolysaccharide-induced lung disease (Zhang Q. et al., 2025), angiotensin-II-induced aortic aneurysm and dissection (Zhang C. et al., 2023), and an in-house T-cell chronic-stimulation model, and harmonised them into a universal peak set of 667,473 chromatin accessibility regions across 48 cell types. Pseudo-bulk differential accessibility analysis with DESeq2 identified opening and closing differentially accessible regions (DARs) per cell type per tissue. HOMER motif enrichment against both non-significant and cell-type-specific stable backgrounds revealed a universal opening programme driven by AP-1 family TFs (Fos, Jun, Atf3, BATF) and a tissue-specific closing programme defined by loss of cell-identity TFs: HNF1b/HNF4a/PPARα in kidney proximal tubule, Smad2/HIF-1α in lung alveolar type-2 cells, KLF4/WT1 in aortic smooth muscle cells, and TCF7/LEF1 in T cells. Cross-referencing disease DARs against the recently published organism-wide mouse aging chromatin atlas (Lu et al., 2026, *Science*) demonstrated that disease-driven opening regions are significantly enriched within aging-associated opening regions, with directional concordance strongest in kidney proximal tubule (Pearson r = 0.45–0.62) and weaker but detectable in lung. These results are consistent with the SIPHON (Stimulus-Induced Programming Hijacks Ontogeny) model of AP-1-driven aging chromatin opening (Patrick et al., 2024) and identify AP-1 as a candidate universal target whose pharmacological inhibition could blunt both ageing- and disease-associated chromatin remodelling. The integrated peak atlas, DAR call set, and analysis code are released as a community resource for cross-tissue regulatory genomics.

**Keywords:** single-cell ATAC-seq, chromatin accessibility, transcription factor, AP-1, aging, age-related disease, differential accessibility, motif enrichment, SIPHON, multi-tissue integration

## Significance Statement

This thesis addresses a long-standing claim in geroscience — that age-related diseases are accelerations rather than orthogonal perturbations of normal aging — at the level of regulatory chromatin. By integrating four mouse scATAC-seq datasets covering kidney ischemia-reperfusion injury, lung COPD, aortic aneurysm and dissection, and chronic T-cell stimulation into a universal peak set of 667,473 regions, and by cross-referencing the resulting disease-DARs against the recently published organism-wide aging chromatin atlas of Lu et al. (2026), I demonstrate three convergent findings.

First, AP-1 family transcription factors drive opening of inflammatory enhancers across all four disease tissues and across all 27 disease-affected cell types tested. Second, tissue-specific cell-identity TFs — HNF/PPARα in kidney, Smad2/HIF-1α in lung, KLF4/WT1 in aorta, TCF7/LEF1 in T cells — lose chromatin accessibility in the same disease cell types, indicating a coupled gain-of-stress and loss-of-identity regulatory programme. Third, the disease opening chromatin programme is significantly enriched within the aging opening chromatin programme, with the strongest directional concordance in kidney proximal tubule (Pearson r = 0.45–0.62), providing chromatin-level support for the SIPHON model of aging chromatin remodelling (Patrick et al., 2024, *Cell Metabolism*).

These findings carry three translational implications. First, AP-1 — already pharmacologically tractable through inhibitors such as T-5224 — is prioritised as a candidate universal target whose inhibition could simultaneously modify aging and disease chromatin trajectories. Second, the disease-aging-shared peak set released as a community resource alongside this thesis enables targeted biomarker development for chromatin-state monitoring of disease progression. Third, the three-layer regulatory model proposed in the discussion — constitutive identity, inducible inflammation, and aging-permissive opening — provides a unifying framework for designing rational combination therapies that simultaneously inhibit AP-1 and restore cell-type identity programmes.

The integrated atlas, the analysis code, and the disease-aging-shared peak track are released as community resources under a Creative Commons CC-BY-4.0 licence.

# Chapter 1 — Introduction

## 1.1 The burden of age-related disease

Population ageing is reshaping the global burden of disease. The number of people aged 65 years or older is projected to more than double, from 761 million in 2021 to 1.6 billion by 2050 (United Nations, 2022), and most non-communicable diseases — cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease (CKD), chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), neurodegeneration, type-2 diabetes, and many cancers — show a strong age dependence in incidence and severity (López-Otín et al., 2013; Niccoli & Partridge, 2012). CKD alone affects an estimated 10% of adults worldwide and remains the seventh leading cause of life-years lost, with most incident cases in adults over 65 (Bikbov et al., 2020; Kalantar-Zadeh et al., 2021). COPD ranked third among all causes of death in 2019 and is overwhelmingly a disease of older smokers (Halpin et al., 2019; GBD Chronic Respiratory Disease Collaborators, 2020). Thoracic aortic aneurysm and aortic dissection (collectively AAD) are diseases of late middle age and beyond, with surgical mortality climbing sharply after age 70 (Isselbacher, 2005; Erbel et al., 2014). Age-related immune decline — most visibly the contraction of the naïve T-cell pool — underlies the increased severity of infections, vaccination failure, and cancer surveillance loss in older adults (Goronzy & Weyand, 2013; Mogilenko et al., 2022).

Across organs and disease entities, three recurring observations have emerged. First, age increases susceptibility to insults that younger tissues tolerate, suggesting a *latent* age-associated vulnerability rather than a single threshold event (Kennedy et al., 2014). Second, advanced lesions in each system show striking phenotypic convergence: parenchymal cells lose their canonical differentiation programme, gain a stress-and-inflammation transcriptional signature, and recruit innate immune cells (Coppé et al., 2010; Acosta et al., 2013). Third, despite the diversity of triggering insults — ischaemia in the kidney, smoke in the lung, mechanical and metabolic stress in the aorta, antigenic persistence in the immune compartment — the *final common pathway* invokes a similar set of transcription factor (TF) families, including AP-1, C/EBP, NF-κB, and STAT (Smale, 2010; Glass & Natoli, 2016; Vierbuchen et al., 2017). These three observations motivate the hypothesis that age-related diseases share a *regulatory* convergence at the chromatin level, even when the upstream stimuli differ.

This thesis pursues that hypothesis with single-cell ATAC-sequencing (scATAC-seq). I integrate four mouse scATAC-seq datasets covering kidney ischemia-reperfusion injury (Muto et al., 2024), COPD-model lung disease (Zhang Q. et al., 2025), angiotensin-II–induced aortic aneurysm and dissection (Zhang C. et al., 2023), and an in-house T-cell chronic-stimulation model, and compare the disease-driven differentially accessible regions (DARs) in each tissue against the organism-wide mouse aging chromatin atlas of Lu et al. (2026, *Science*), interpreting overlap with reference to the SIPHON conceptual framework of Patrick et al. (2024, *Cell Metabolism*). Throughout, I treat *chromatin accessibility* as the readout, and *transcription factor binding motifs* as the inferred causal currency.

### 1.1.0 Clinical and economic burden of the four diseases studied here

The four diseases analysed in this thesis between them account for an outsized share of healthcare expenditure and disability-adjusted life years lost (DALYs) in aged populations. Globally, CKD is responsible for ~3 % of all DALYs and is increasing faster than almost any other non-communicable disease in absolute terms; in Australia, CKD prevalence in adults over 65 is estimated at 35 %, with diabetes and hypertension as the leading aetiological drivers (Bikbov et al., 2020; Kalantar-Zadeh et al., 2021; AIHW, 2023). Acute kidney injury (AKI), the precursor to a substantial fraction of incident CKD cases, occurs in ~13 % of hospitalised adults over 65 and triples the risk of progressing to dialysis over the following five years (Hsu et al., 2009; Bonventre & Yang, 2011).

COPD is the third-leading cause of death worldwide, with an age-standardised mortality of ~45 per 100,000 person-years; in low- and middle-income countries the mortality is higher because of biomass-fuel exposure in addition to cigarette smoke (Halpin et al., 2019; GBD Chronic Respiratory Disease Collaborators, 2020). The cost burden in older COPD patients is dominated by exacerbations, which are themselves linked to a "chronic-on-acute" inflammatory-chromatin-remodelling phenotype well-captured by the mouse COPD model analysed here (Zhang Q. et al., 2025).

Thoracic aortic aneurysm and dissection have a population-wide incidence of ~10 per 100,000 person-years but a case fatality approaching 50 % when dissection occurs unmonitored; the disease is almost exclusively a disease of adults over 50 (Isselbacher, 2005; Erbel et al., 2014; Pirruccello et al., 2022). Sporadic AAD (the form modelled in Zhang C. et al., 2023) is driven by mechanical and metabolic stress on the aortic wall and is genetically distinct from the rarer Marfan-syndrome forms.

Immune aging — the contraction of the naïve T-cell pool, the expansion of memory and exhausted populations, and the gradual erosion of vaccine-responsive T-cell repertoires — does not cause disease directly but multiplies the severity and mortality of infections, cancers, and vaccine failure in older adults (Goronzy & Weyand, 2013; Mogilenko et al., 2022). The chronic-stimulation T-cell model analysed here mimics persistent antigenic stimulation and serves as an in-vitro analogue of T-cell exhaustion, a phenotype that intersects with immune aging at the chromatin level (Wherry, 2011; Pauken et al., 2016; Mognol et al., 2017).

Taken together, the four diseases analysed here cover three solid organs and one diffuse immune compartment, are responsible for a substantial share of healthcare burden in aged populations, and represent the canonical disease models for which the geroscience claim of "age-related diseases as accelerated aging" has the strongest face validity. The chromatin-level analysis presented in this thesis tests that claim quantitatively.

### 1.1.1 Why scATAC-seq is the right lens for studying age-related disease

Age-related diseases pose three analytical challenges that conventional bulk-tissue approaches cannot resolve. First, the disease-relevant cell populations — failed-repair proximal tubule cells in CKD, transitional alveolar epithelial cells in COPD, synthetic-state smooth muscle cells in aortic disease, exhausted T cells in chronic immune activation — are typically minority populations within the parent tissue and are diluted into invisibility by bulk-tissue assays (Kirita et al., 2020; Habermann et al., 2020). Second, the regulatory state of these cells, not their abundance, is the disease-relevant readout: a 5 % minority of failed-repair PT cells with rewired chromatin is more clinically consequential than a 70 % majority of intact tubular epithelium (Muto et al., 2024). Third, the temporal trajectory of disease progression is not simply a quantitative ramp of one signal but a qualitative reorganisation of cell-type-specific regulatory programmes, which requires per-cell rather than per-tissue resolution (Kirita et al., 2020; Strunz et al., 2020).

Single-cell ATAC-sequencing addresses all three challenges. It resolves chromatin accessibility at thousands to millions of cis-regulatory elements per individual cell, identifies disease-affected minority populations within their physiological context, and provides per-cell-type trajectory information when combined with longitudinal sampling. Compared to single-cell RNA-sequencing, scATAC-seq has the additional advantage of directly measuring the regulatory layer at which transcription factor activity is encoded (Buenrostro et al., 2013; Cusanovich et al., 2015; Satpathy et al., 2019). This thesis exploits these properties to map the regulatory architecture of four age-related disease processes at single-cell resolution.

## 1.2 Aging as a chromatin phenomenon

Aging is increasingly framed as a regulatory rather than a degenerative disease. The classical hallmarks of aging — genomic instability, telomere attrition, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence — are accompanied, and to a large extent driven, by an "epigenetic hallmark" comprising progressive DNA methylation drift, loss of heterochromatin, redistribution of histone modifications, and changes in chromatin accessibility (López-Otín et al., 2013, 2023; Sen et al., 2016; Booth & Brunet, 2016). Aged cells from blood, muscle, brain, kidney, and skin all display reduced repressive histone modifications (H3K9me3, H3K27me3) at constitutive heterochromatin, increased accessibility at intergenic enhancers, and broadened active chromatin states at lineage-defining loci (Pal & Tyler, 2016; Yang et al., 2023; Liu Z. et al., 2022).

A second body of work has shown that *aging-associated chromatin changes are not random*. Two principles have emerged. First, aging tends to open regions associated with inflammation, stress response, and senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) effector genes (Coppé et al., 2010; Acosta et al., 2013; Tasdemir et al., 2016). Second, aging tends to close regions encoding cell-type identity programmes — for example, AT2 surfactant programmes in the lung, tubular programmes in the kidney, and contractile programmes in vascular smooth muscle cells (Wang et al., 2022; Buisine et al., 2021; Patrick et al., 2024). The aged genome therefore drifts in a *predictable* direction: away from highly specialised cell-identity states and towards a generic inflammatory-permissive state.

Two recent landmark studies crystallised this picture. First, Patrick et al. (2024, *Cell Metabolism*) proposed the **SIPHON** (**S**timulus-**I**nduced **P**rogramming **H**ijacks **O**ntoge**N**y) model, in which AP-1-linked chromatin opening at late-life *cis*-regulatory elements progressively reveals additional transcription factor binding sites that compete for AP-1 co-factors initially bound at early-life regulatory elements; the result is a re-deployment of developmental regulatory grammar in the aged organism. Second, Lu et al. (2026, *Science*) profiled chromatin accessibility in more than 10 million nuclei across 21 mouse tissues at three age groups (1, 5, and 21 months) using *EasySci-ATAC*, providing the largest organism-wide aging chromatin atlas to date and explicitly mapping aging-associated chromatin remodelling, AP-1-motif gain, and identity-TF loss across 536 main cell types and 1,828 finer-grained subtypes. Together, the Patrick framework and the Lu atlas establish that aging chromatin opens reproducibly at AP-1-bound enhancers, expands an innate-immune signature, and erodes lineage-specific chromatin identity. The core regulatory hubs — AP-1 family TFs Fos, Jun, Atf3, and BATF — are pharmacologically tractable.

The SIPHON model and the Lu atlas together raise a precise, testable question for this thesis. **If aging predisposes tissues to a stereotypic chromatin programme, does superimposed disease *accelerate* that programme along the same trajectory, or does it engage a parallel, disease-specific trajectory?** If diseases ride the aging trajectory, then disease and aging DARs should be (i) significantly overlapping in genomic space and (ii) directionally concordant (i.e., regions opening in disease should also open with age, and vice versa). This thesis tests this prediction by intersecting disease DARs from three age-related disease models (kidney, lung, aorta) and one chronic-immune-stimulation model (T cells) against the cell-type-matched aging DARs derived from the Lu et al. (2026) atlas.

### 1.2.1 Chromatin states define the cellular memory of aging

A useful conceptualisation that has emerged in the last decade is that chromatin states encode a *cellular memory* whose drift over time is functionally equivalent to aging itself (Sen et al., 2016; Booth & Brunet, 2016; Yang et al., 2023). Three lines of evidence support this view. First, ectopic reactivation of Yamanaka-factor expression in aged cells partially restores youthful gene expression and tissue function, even in the absence of full reprogramming, indicating that chromatin state — not genotype — limits aged-cell function (Lu et al., 2020; Browder et al., 2022). Second, the *Information Theory of Aging* of Yang et al. (2023) shows that experimentally inducing DNA double-strand breaks, which preserve sequence integrity but force chromatin reorganisation, recapitulates many hallmarks of physiological aging in young mice. Third, recent multi-omic atlases — including the Lu et al. (2026) organism-wide aging chromatin atlas analysed in this thesis — demonstrate that chromatin trajectories are highly cell-type-resolved and reproducible across animals, suggesting that aging-associated chromatin change is regulated rather than stochastic (Lu et al., 2026; Patrick et al., 2024).

The convergence of these lines of evidence has reframed aging from a passive accumulation of damage to an *active regulatory programme* whose drivers are, in principle, identifiable and therapeutically tractable. The central goal of geroscience is now to identify the upstream regulators of aging-chromatin remodelling and to test whether their inhibition extends healthspan (Kennedy et al., 2014). AP-1, the focus of this thesis, is the leading candidate.

## 1.3 Transcription factor control of accessible chromatin

A transcription factor's footprint in the genome is captured by two related but distinct quantities. The first is *binding* — measurable by ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN — which depends on the presence of the TF protein, post-translational modifications, and cofactor availability. The second is *accessibility at the binding motif* — measurable by ATAC-seq — which reflects whether the binding site is sterically available within an accessible nucleosome-free region. The two are not equivalent: a motif may be accessible without a bound TF, and conversely an inaccessible motif rarely has stable TF binding. But across thousands of genome-scale comparisons in the ENCODE and IHEC consortia, accessibility around a motif and TF occupancy at that motif co-vary tightly when the motif lies within a peak (ENCODE Project Consortium, 2012; Buenrostro et al., 2013; Kundaje et al., 2015).

This co-variation makes scATAC-seq an effective surrogate for TF activity. By measuring accessibility per cell at hundreds of thousands of regulatory elements simultaneously, scATAC-seq provides per-cell-type estimates of which TF motifs are differentially accessible between conditions. Motif enrichment analysis tools — most prominently HOMER (Heinz et al., 2010) — formalise this by computing the over-representation of TF binding motifs in a set of accessible regions of interest relative to a matched background. When opening DARs are enriched for AP-1 motifs, the parsimonious interpretation is that AP-1 family TFs have driven the chromatin opening in those regions (Vierbuchen et al., 2017; Madsen et al., 2018).

Three TF families dominate the aging-disease literature and recur in this thesis. **AP-1**, a heterodimer or homodimer of basic leucine zipper (bZIP) proteins (Fos, Jun, Atf, BATF, JDP families), is the canonical immediate-early stress-response TF (Eferl & Wagner, 2003; Hess et al., 2004). AP-1 is constitutively bound to a large fraction of stress-responsive enhancers and acts as a "pioneer-like" factor that opens chromatin in collaboration with PU.1 in macrophages, with C/EBP in adipocytes, and with stimulus-specific factors in stressed cells (Heinz et al., 2010; Phanstiel et al., 2017; Yamashita & Iwama, 2022). **Cell-identity TFs** — for example, HNF1b/HNF4a/PPARα in tubular kidney epithelium (Lazzeri et al., 2019; Marable et al., 2018), Smad2/HIF-1α in alveolar epithelium (Zacharias et al., 2018), and KLF4/WT1 in vascular smooth muscle (Wirka et al., 2019; Shankman et al., 2015) — define the canonical differentiation state of each cell type. Their accessibility at cell-identity enhancers is lost when cells de-differentiate. **CTCF and architectural factors** define topological domain boundaries and are largely invariant across cell types and conditions (Dixon et al., 2012; Phillips & Corces, 2009); their persistence serves as an internal QC for the motif enrichment analysis and they appear, as expected, in stable background sets in this thesis.

The interplay between AP-1 (universal stress) and identity TFs (cell-specific) is the central regulatory tension addressed in Aim 2 of this thesis.

### 1.3.1 The pioneer-factor concept and the AP-1 dimer code

Two refinements of the basic TF-binding picture are essential for interpreting the chromatin-level findings of this thesis. The first is the *pioneer-factor concept*, originally formalised in the FoxA and OCT4 literature (Cirillo et al., 2002; Zaret & Carroll, 2011): a subset of TFs can engage their motifs even when the local chromatin is nucleosome-occluded, and their binding triggers local nucleosome eviction, recruitment of histone acetyltransferases, and subsequent accessibility increase. Classical pioneer factors include FoxA, OCT4, Pax7, and KLF4; AP-1 is now recognised as a *pioneer-like* or *facultative pioneer* factor that does not initiate accessibility de novo at all sites but can open chromatin at a subset of latent enhancers when stimulated (Vierbuchen et al., 2017; Madsen et al., 2018). This pioneer-like behaviour explains why AP-1 motif enrichment in opening DARs (a recurrent finding in this thesis) is causally consistent with AP-1 driving the opening rather than passively following it.

The second refinement is the *AP-1 dimer code*. AP-1 is not a single protein but a heterodimer composed of one member of each of the bZIP subfamilies: Fos (Fos, Fra1, Fra2, FosB), Jun (c-Jun, JunB, JunD), Atf (Atf2, Atf3, Atf4, Atf6, Atf7), BATF (BATF, BATF2, BATF3), or JDP (JDP2) (Eferl & Wagner, 2003; Hess et al., 2004). The 15 possible dimer combinations have overlapping but distinguishable DNA-binding preferences and very different transcriptional outcomes — JunB-FosB dimers, for example, are oncogenic in some contexts but anti-proliferative in others. HOMER motif enrichment cannot distinguish between dimer compositions because the canonical AP-1 motif TGAC/GTCA is shared by all dimer permutations. Pinpointing the specific AP-1 dimer driving a given disease chromatin opening requires CUT&RUN or ChIP-seq for individual subunits — an extension beyond the scope of this thesis but a logical follow-up for the most biologically interesting cell types identified here (kidney PT, lung AT2, aortic SMC, chronically stimulated T cells).

## 1.4 Single-cell ATAC-seq: principles and analysis

ATAC-seq exploits the hyperactive Tn5 transposase to fragment accessible chromatin and simultaneously ligate sequencing adapters (Buenrostro et al., 2013). In bulk ATAC-seq, millions of cells are pooled, and the readout is an aggregate accessibility profile averaging across all cell types in the input. Bulk ATAC-seq misses cell-type-resolved signal — a critical limitation for tissues such as kidney, lung, and aorta in which the disease-relevant cell types are minority populations. scATAC-seq, by contrast, tags individual cells with a unique cell barcode, allowing per-cell accessibility profiles to be reconstructed. Two technological platforms dominate: combinatorial-indexing sci-ATAC-seq (Cusanovich et al., 2015) and droplet-based 10x Genomics Chromium scATAC (Satpathy et al., 2019). All four disease datasets in this thesis used 10x Chromium chemistry, aligned with Cell Ranger ATAC to mm10; the Lu et al. (2026) aging reference dataset used the combinatorial-indexing *EasySci-ATAC* chemistry to achieve 10-million-nucleus throughput.

The data type produced by scATAC-seq is a sparse cell-by-peak fragment-count matrix. The matrix is binarised or normalised, then passed through an LSI/SVD dimensionality reduction (Stuart et al., 2021) before clustering and UMAP embedding. Two analysis families dominate downstream: (i) per-cell or per-cluster motif activity, computed by tools such as ChromVAR (Schep et al., 2017); and (ii) pseudo-bulk differential accessibility, in which fragment counts are aggregated per cell type per sample and analysed with bulk RNA-seq tools such as DESeq2 (Love et al., 2014). Pseudo-bulk DAR analysis is the workhorse for cohort-scale comparisons because it correctly inflates standard errors for low-cell-count cell types and supports the standard log fold change / adjusted p-value framework (Squair et al., 2021).

**Peak calling under transposase-shifted reads.** MACS2 (Zhang Y. et al., 2008) is the dominant peak caller for scATAC-seq, but its default parameters were optimised for ChIP-seq, in which reads are produced by immunoprecipitation rather than transposase tagmentation. The Tn5 transposase inserts a 9-bp staggered cut, requiring a +4/−5 read-shift correction to centre fragments on the true accessibility site (Buenrostro et al., 2013). The MACS2 parameters used in this thesis (`--shift -73 --extsize 146 --nomodel --keep-dup all -q 0.05`) follow the recommendations of Buenrostro et al. (2015) and Stuart et al. (2021) for transposase-based reads: the −73 shift and 146 extsize together centre a 146-bp peak window on each fragment, matching the canonical nucleosome footprint and producing peaks with consistent biological interpretation.

**Latent semantic indexing for chromatin dimensionality reduction.** Unlike scRNA-seq, scATAC-seq matrices are intrinsically more binary and sparser, with most cells having either 0 or 1 fragment at most peaks. Standard PCA tends to over-fit to highly accessible peaks (which dominate the variance) at the expense of the cell-type-resolving information carried by the long tail of moderately accessible peaks. Latent semantic indexing (LSI), originally developed for text mining, treats the cell-by-peak matrix as a term-document matrix, applies TF-IDF normalisation to down-weight ubiquitous peaks, and computes singular value decomposition on the normalised matrix (Cusanovich et al., 2015; Stuart et al., 2021). The first LSI component typically captures sequencing-depth variation and is discarded, with subsequent components used for clustering and UMAP. ArchR and Signac both implement LSI as the default scATAC-seq dimensionality reduction (Granja et al., 2021; Stuart et al., 2021).

**Gene-activity scores as a chromatin-derived expression proxy.** Although scATAC-seq does not directly measure transcription, the per-cell summed accessibility across a gene body and its proximal promoter — the "gene activity score" — correlates well with scRNA-seq expression of the same gene and is widely used for marker-gene-based cell-type annotation in pure scATAC-seq datasets (Pliner et al., 2018; Stuart et al., 2021). In this thesis, gene-activity scores were used to annotate cell types prior to validation by `CoveragePlot()` at canonical marker loci (Figures 7 and 8).

Several technical and conceptual challenges shape the analysis. First, *peak set choice* matters profoundly: peak sets called separately per condition can systematically over- or under-call differential accessibility because the underlying interval definitions differ (Granja et al., 2021; Bravo González-Blas et al., 2023). The remedy adopted here is a *universal consensus peak set* built by extending all sample-specific MACS2 summits ±250 bp and applying the iterative greedy merging procedure implemented in pycisTopic's `get_consensus_peaks` (Bravo González-Blas et al., 2023), giving 667,473 peaks across the four tissues analysed. Second, *fragment file linkage* is required for Signac-based visualisation of accessibility coverage, and the four datasets use slightly different barcode conventions that must be reconciled (Stuart et al., 2021). Third, *cell-type annotation* in scATAC-seq is harder than in scRNA-seq because peaks do not map one-to-one to genes; we annotate by gene-activity score at canonical marker loci and validate with coverage plots (Fig. 8).

## 1.5 Cross-tissue integration as a discovery strategy

Single-tissue scATAC-seq atlases now exist for kidney (Muto et al., 2021; Muto et al., 2024), lung (Wang et al., 2020; Strunz et al., 2020), aorta (Wirka et al., 2019), and many other systems. Each atlas has delivered tissue-specific biology, but very few studies have attempted *cross-tissue regulatory integration* (with the notable exceptions of Cusanovich et al., 2018 in young mice; Patrick et al., 2024 across multiple cell types; and Lu et al., 2026 at organism scale across 21 tissues and three age groups). The barriers are practical — peak coordinates differ between studies, cell-type vocabularies differ, sample sizes differ — and conceptual: it is not obvious that comparing kidney and aortic chromatin in disease is informative, because the cells and physiology are so different.

This thesis takes the position that cross-tissue integration is precisely the strategy needed to *separate generic disease signals from tissue-specific signals*. By analysing four diseases with diverse aetiology under a single peak set, with a single motif enrichment pipeline, and against a single aging reference, the universal versus tissue-specific regulatory programmes can be parsed directly. A motif that appears in opening DARs across all four tissues is more likely to be a general disease driver than a motif that appears only in one tissue. Conversely, a motif that appears only in kidney PT or only in lung AT2 is more likely to be tissue-specific identity loss. The same logic underlies the design of the human ENCODE3 cross-tissue analysis (Moore et al., 2020) and the recent multi-tissue human aging atlas of Liu et al. (2023).

## 1.6 Hypothesis, aims, and significance

**Central hypothesis.** Age-related diseases across kidney, lung, aorta, and immune compartments share a convergent chromatin-remodelling programme in which AP-1 family TFs drive disease-associated opening, while loss of tissue-specific cell-identity TFs drives disease-associated closing. This convergent programme overlaps significantly with the aging chromatin programme observed by Lu et al. (2026) and interpreted within the SIPHON framework of Patrick et al. (2024), positioning disease-driven chromatin remodelling as an *acceleration* of normal aging rather than an orthogonal process.

### 2.1.1 Tissue dissociation, nuclei isolation, and 10x Chromium loading

Tissue dissociation protocols are dataset-specific because each tissue requires a different combination of enzymatic and mechanical disruption to release single nuclei suitable for 10x Chromium loading. For the kidney IRI dataset, Muto et al. (2024) used a cold-isotonic-buffer protocol with light mechanical disruption to minimise the introduction of dissociation-associated stress transcripts; for the lung COPD dataset, Zhang Q. et al. (2025) used a collagenase-and-dispase dissociation followed by red-blood-cell lysis; for the aorta AAD dataset, Zhang C. et al. (2023) used a multi-enzyme cocktail of collagenase II, elastase, and DNase I to digest the aortic wall extracellular matrix; for the in-house T-cell dataset, the Patrick laboratory used a standard splenic dissociation followed by Percoll-gradient lymphocyte enrichment.

Each dissociation protocol introduces its own technical biases — collagenase digestion can over-represent extracellular-matrix-producing populations such as fibroblasts and underrepresent shear-sensitive populations such as endothelial cells; cold-buffer protocols preserve baseline chromatin states better than warm-buffer protocols but at the cost of lower cell yield. These biases are not removable post hoc and must be acknowledged when comparing cell-type representations across the four datasets in this thesis. To minimise their impact, all downstream analyses (DAR calls, motif enrichment, aging comparison) are performed *per cell type per tissue* rather than at the aggregate-tissue level, so that biases affecting cell-type *abundance* do not propagate to per-cell-type *chromatin state*.

Following dissociation, single nuclei were loaded onto a 10x Chromium controller targeting 8,000–10,000 nuclei recovered per channel. Library construction, sequencing, and base-calling followed the standard 10x Chromium scATAC-seq v1.1 protocol with paired-end 50-bp + 50-bp reads at a target of 25,000 read pairs per nucleus (Buenrostro et al., 2015; Stuart et al., 2021). Raw FASTQ files were processed with Cell Ranger ATAC v2.1.0 against the mm10 reference genome with default parameters.

## 2.2 Preprocessing and quality control

Per-sample preprocessing followed the Stuart et al. (2021) Signac vignette with project-specific modifications. Cell Ranger ATAC fragment files (`fragments.tsv.gz`) and per-cell barcode metrics were ingested into ArchR v1.0.2 (Granja et al., 2021) and Signac v1.10 (Stuart et al., 2021) Seurat-compatible objects. Cells were filtered using the standard thresholds: TSS enrichment > 4, nucleosome signal < 4, fragments per cell > 1,000 and < 100,000, and blacklist ratio < 0.05 (ENCODE blacklist v2; Amemiya et al., 2019). Doublets were removed with AMULET (Thibodeau et al., 2021) at the default 0.05 q-value threshold.

Per-cell QC metrics across the four tissues are shown in Figure 4. Median fragments per cell ranged from 7,200 (T cells) to 18,400 (kidney); median TSS enrichment ranged from 6.1 (aorta) to 11.3 (kidney); median nucleosome signal was 0.55 to 1.2 across all tissues, well within the recommended ≤4 range. All tissues passed standard QC, allowing downstream pseudo-bulk DAR analysis to proceed with adequate fragment coverage per cell type.

## 2.3 Universal peak set construction

A universal peak set across all four tissues was constructed using the consensus-peak procedure of Bravo González-Blas et al. (2023, *Nat. Methods*) implemented in pycisTopic v1.0.3. The procedure consists of three steps. First, per-sample peak calls were generated with MACS2 v2.2.7.1 (Zhang et al., 2008) using parameters `--nomodel --shift -73 --extsize 146 -q 0.05 --keep-dup all`, appropriate for Tn5-transposed scATAC fragments (Buenrostro et al., 2015). Second, summits from all samples were extended by ±250 bp to give 501-bp fixed-width candidate peaks. Third, candidate peaks were iteratively merged with the `get_consensus_peaks` greedy algorithm: at each iteration the highest-scoring peak was retained and overlapping lower-scoring peaks were discarded, until no overlaps remained. The procedure was first applied within each tissue (giving 581,481 kidney, 245,604 lung, 230,473 aorta, and 145,370 T-cell peaks), and then across the four tissue-level consensus sets to give a universal peak set of **667,473 fixed-width peaks**. The universal peak set is used for all downstream cross-tissue comparisons (Figures 5 and 6).

## 2.4 Cell-type annotation

Cell-type annotation was performed per tissue using a combination of (i) unsupervised Leiden clustering on the LSI embedding (Stuart et al., 2021), (ii) gene-activity scores at canonical marker genes via Signac `GeneActivity()` and validated with `CoveragePlot()`, and (iii) label transfer from matched scRNA-seq references where available (Stuart et al., 2019). Kidney annotation used the Muto et al. (2024) reference scRNA-seq labels; lung annotation used the Zhang et al. (2025) labels; aorta annotation followed Wirka et al. (2019); T cells were sub-clustered de novo with `FindAllMarkers()` and annotated against canonical T-cell markers (Cd4, Cd8a, Foxp3, Tcf7, Lef1, Klrg1, Ikzf2). A Ebf1+ B-cell contaminant cluster was identified in the T-cell dataset and excluded from downstream analysis. Final cell-type counts and marker-gene coverage plots are shown in Figures 7 and 8.

## 2.5 Pseudo-bulk DAR analysis

Differential accessibility was computed per cell type per contrast using pseudo-bulk DESeq2 (Love et al., 2014) following the recommendations of Squair et al. (2021). For each cell type, fragment counts were summed per sample to yield a peak × sample matrix; DESeq2's negative-binomial generalised linear model was then fit with sample as the unit of replication. The standard pseudo-bulk approach was used rather than ChromVAR or wilcoxon-on-single-cells because the former correctly inflates standard errors for cell types with few samples (Squair et al., 2021).

Three tissue-specific configurations were applied. **Kidney** used three hardcoded contrasts (`Day14 vs Sham`, `Day42 vs Sham`, `Day42 vs Day14`) with a minimum of 80 cells per group, and an expression-threshold filter of 0.05 (a peak must be present in ≥5% of cells in at least one group). Eligible cell types were DCT, Endothelial, IC, Macrophages, PC, PT, Podocytes, and TAL. **Lung** used `Case vs Control` with a minimum of 50 cells per group. **Aorta** used `Challenge vs Control` with a minimum of 80 cells per group. **T cells** used all pairwise contrasts among five conditions (Young_chronic, Young_control, Young_acute, Juvenile, Aged) with `make_contrasts()` automatic pairwise generation, minimum 80 cells per group.

Significant DARs were defined by `padj < 0.05` and `|log2FC| > 0.5`. Opening DARs are those with positive log2FC (more accessible in disease/aged); closing DARs are those with negative log2FC. DAR counts per cell type are summarised in Figure 9. Scripts: `Kidney_pseudo-bulk_DAR.R`, `Aorta_Lung_Tcells_pseudo-bulk_DAR.R`.

## 2.6 HOMER motif enrichment

TF motif enrichment in opening and closing DARs was computed with HOMER's `findMotifsGenome.pl` (Heinz et al., 2010) against the mm10 reference. Two complementary background sets were used to control for genomic-distribution biases:

1. **NS background.** All peaks tested by DESeq2 but not reaching significance (`padj ≥ 0.05`). This background controls for accessibility-level biases but not for cell-type-specific peak architecture.
2. **Stable background.** Cell-type-specific stable peaks defined as those accessible in both conditions but not differentially accessible. This background controls for both accessibility and cell-type-specific peak architecture and is the more conservative comparison.

For each cell-type-by-contrast combination, four motif enrichment tests were performed: Opening-vs-NS, Closing-vs-NS, Opening-vs-Stable, Closing-vs-Stable. A minimum of 20 peaks per query set was required; cell-type-by-contrast combinations falling below this threshold were excluded. HOMER's *de novo* and *known* motif scans were both run; *known* motif results are used throughout the thesis for cross-comparability. The HOMER known-motif database (v4.11.1) includes 426 vertebrate TF motifs derived from curated ChIP-seq peak sets, providing a stable comparison surface across cell types and tissues; *de novo* motifs were inspected for the top three opening cell types of each tissue to confirm that the dominant *de novo* motif consensus matches the dominant *known* motif (AP-1 in all four tissues). The 20-peak minimum per query set was chosen empirically: at smaller query sizes HOMER's hypergeometric test becomes unstable and motif enrichment p-values become inflated, an issue documented in the HOMER tutorial and confirmed by sensitivity analysis on the kidney IRI Day 14 vs Sham dataset (data not shown). Output −log10(P-value) values were signed by direction of enrichment (positive for opening, negative for closing) for visualisation in the integrated heatmap (Figure 10). A *cell-type identity TF baseline* (Figure 11) was generated by running HOMER on cell-type-specific stable peaks against a genome-wide random background, capturing canonical identity TFs for each cell type. Scripts: `run_HOMER_closing_vs_opening.sh`, `run_HOMER_overlapping_peaks.sh`, `plot_HOMER_overlapping.R`, `Integration_heatmap.R`.

## 2.7 Aging DAR comparison (Aim 3)

The aging reference was the Lu et al. (2026) *Science* organism-wide mouse aging chromatin atlas (`EasySci-ATAC`, 10 million nuclei across 21 tissues at 1, 5, and 21 months of age; data portal `https://epiage.net/`). Aging DARs were re-called from the raw aging atlas data using the same pseudo-bulk DESeq2 pipeline as for disease, for matched cell types only (kidney PT, TAL, DCT, PC, Macrophages; lung AT2, B, Ciliated, EC-vasc, Eosinophils, Fib, Mac-alv, Mac-inter, NK, Pen, SMCs, T) with the contrast `Aged (21 mo) vs Young (1 mo)`. This re-call (rather than using published aging DAR tables) ensures that disease and aging DARs are defined over the *same universal peak set* and *same statistical thresholds*, enabling direct intersection.

Genomic overlap was computed with `bedtools intersect -wa -wb` (Quinlan & Hall, 2010). For each cell type, disease-significant peaks (`padj < 0.05`, `|log2FC| > 0.5`) were intersected against the full aging peak set carrying log2FC and padj annotations. Each overlapping pair contributes one (disease log2FC, aging log2FC) data point to the scatter plot (Figure 20).

Two statistical tests were applied. First, **Fisher's exact test** (one-sided, alternative = "greater") was used to test for over-representation of disease DARs within aging DARs. The 2 × 2 contingency table was constructed as: `a` = peaks in both disease-DAR and aging-DAR sets; `b` = peaks in disease-DAR but not aging-DAR; `c` = peaks in aging-DAR but not disease-DAR; `d` = remaining peaks in the universal set. Aging significance was relaxed to `padj < 0.2` for Fisher's testing only, to maintain test power in cell types with few aging-significant peaks (Figure 19, Panel A). Second, **Pearson correlation** was computed between disease log2FC and aging log2FC restricted to the aging-significant overlap subset (`a_padj < 0.05`) to give a directional concordance estimate (Figure 19 Panel B, Figure 20). Scripts: `03_overlap_and_plots.sh` (in `sciencepaper_DAR_comparison/`), `04_region_classification.sh`.

## 2.9 Choice of DAR-calling strategy: pseudo-bulk vs. single-cell statistics

The choice between pseudo-bulk DESeq2 and per-cell statistical methods (Wilcoxon, Tobit regression, ChromVAR) is consequential for the false-discovery rate of scATAC-seq DAR analysis (Squair et al., 2021). Per-cell methods are computationally efficient and provide single-cell resolution but systematically inflate false-positive rates because they treat individual cells as independent replicates, ignoring the biological replicate structure (the unit of biological replication is the *animal*, not the *cell*). For a study with 3 animals per group and 1,000 cells per animal, per-cell Wilcoxon produces standard errors that are too small by a factor of ≈√1000, leading to systematic false positives at any meaningful effect-size threshold. Pseudo-bulk methods correct this by aggregating fragment counts per animal per cell type before fitting the GLM, restoring the correct unit of replication.

A second consideration is the choice of statistical model. DESeq2 (Love et al., 2014) fits a negative-binomial GLM with empirical-Bayes shrinkage of dispersion estimates, which is well calibrated for the count-based distribution of pseudo-bulk fragment counts. EdgeR (Robinson et al., 2010) and limma-voom (Law et al., 2014) are reasonable alternatives but were not used here for consistency with the Stuart et al. (2021) Signac vignette and the prior literature in chromatin-accessibility DAR analysis. ChromVAR (Schep et al., 2017) computes per-cell *motif activity scores* rather than per-peak differential accessibility and is useful as a complementary analysis but does not produce the same DAR set required for cross-tissue comparison or genomic intersection with the aging atlas. In this thesis, pseudo-bulk DESeq2 was chosen as the primary DAR method, with ChromVAR motif scores reserved for sensitivity analysis (data not shown).

## 2.10 Minimum cell counts and sensitivity analyses

The minimum-cell-count thresholds per group (80 kidney, 50 lung, 80 aorta, 80 T cells) were chosen to balance two competing concerns. On the lower side, fewer than ~50 cells per group produces unreliable pseudo-bulk fragment counts because too few cells contribute to each peak; high-accessibility peaks become saturated and low-accessibility peaks become indistinguishable from noise. On the upper side, applying a uniform 100-cell threshold to all tissues would have excluded several biologically important cell populations from the lung dataset, which has the smallest cell counts. The 50-cell threshold for lung was a project-specific exception negotiated with the supervisor on grounds of biological priority.

To verify that the thresholds do not introduce systematic bias, I performed a sensitivity analysis on the kidney PT compartment (the largest cell-type input). Re-running the DAR analysis at thresholds of 60, 80, 100, and 150 cells per group produced DAR set sizes of 14,212, 13,476, 12,896, and 11,540 respectively (`padj < 0.05`, `|log2FC| > 0.5`). The intersect of the four DAR sets covered ≥85 % of the smallest set in all cases, indicating that the DAR call is stable across reasonable threshold variation. The 80-cell threshold was chosen as a balance between recovering DARs with adequate statistical power and excluding noisy low-cell-count contrasts.

## 2.11 Code organisation and reproducibility infrastructure

Analysis scripts are organised by aim and by tissue. Each script is parameterised so that the same code can be run on any of the four tissues by setting the `tissue` variable at the top. Per-cell-type subprocesses are dispatched as parallel jobs via a shell wrapper, allowing the full DAR pipeline (Aim 2) to complete in ~6 hours on a 32-core compute node. The HOMER motif enrichment runs are the rate-limiting step, requiring ~20 minutes per cell type per direction; total HOMER runtime across all four tissues was ~25 hours on the project's allocated cluster.

All random-seed-dependent steps use `set.seed(2024)` for R and `random_state=2024` for Python. Each script writes both its inputs and outputs to a fixed directory structure under `/scratch/yanchen-thesis/` (now archived to the project's research computing storage), enabling step-by-step re-runs without recomputing upstream products. The intermediate-data archive (~140 GB) is mirrored to Zenodo as part of the data-availability statement (Appendix B).

### 3.1.5 Per-tissue peak density and accessibility distributions

To further characterise the universal peak set, I examined the per-tissue peak density (peaks per Mb of genome) and the per-cell mean accessibility (fragments per peak per cell). Kidney shows the highest peak density (~225 peaks per Mb), reflecting both the larger sample size and the broader cell-type diversity. T cells show the lowest density (~56 peaks per Mb), consistent with the more restricted regulatory programme of the lymphoid lineage. Per-cell mean accessibility (after universal-peak intersection) is comparable across tissues at 2,400–3,100 fragments per cell within annotated peaks, indicating that the universal peak set captures the bulk of the accessible chromatin signal in all four datasets.

A useful diagnostic for the universal peak set is the *peak-recall rate*: the fraction of per-tissue peaks recovered when projecting back from the universal set. Across the four tissues, peak recall ranges from 91.2 % (T cells) to 94.7 % (kidney), confirming that the iterative merging procedure in pycisTopic (Bravo González-Blas et al., 2023) preserves the per-tissue peak structure within ≈5 % accuracy. The lost peaks are predominantly low-signal sample-specific peaks below the universal-peak merging threshold; these are recovered in per-tissue analyses (Supplementary Figure 4) and excluded only from the cross-tissue universal-peak DAR analyses where consistency takes priority.

### 3.1.6 Cohort-level batch effects are minor relative to biological signal

A potential concern in any integrated multi-dataset analysis is batch effect — systematic technical variation that masquerades as biological signal. To assess batch contamination, I computed silhouette scores comparing cluster membership to (i) cell type, (ii) tissue, (iii) condition (disease vs control), and (iv) original dataset. The silhouette is strongest for tissue and cell type (silhouette ~0.55 and ~0.42 respectively) and weakest for original dataset (silhouette ~0.08), indicating that biological signal dominates over batch in the integrated dataset. Within each tissue, the per-condition silhouette is intermediate (~0.18–0.28), reflecting that the chromatin state of each disease cell type is distinct from but recognisably related to its control counterpart, as expected for a chronic disease model.

In aggregate, Aim 1 delivers a quality-controlled, integrated scATAC-seq cohort of 103,663 cells across 42 cell types and a universal peak set of 667,473 regions — the analytical substrate for the cross-tissue motif and aging-comparison analyses that follow.

### 3.2.8.1 The T-cell exhaustion programme is preserved at the chromatin level across multiple T-cell subtypes

To further dissect the chronic-stimulation T-cell response, I examined DARs per T-cell sub-population separately (Figure 16 and Supplementary Figure 3). The Young_chronic vs Young_control contrast produces detectable DARs in every T-cell subtype with adequate cell count (memory T cell, effector T cell, CD8+ effector, cycling T cell, and Treg), but the magnitude of the AP-1 opening response is largest in the cycling and CD8+ effector subtypes, mirroring the published finding that exhaustion-associated chromatin remodelling is concentrated in the proliferating effector compartment (Wherry, 2011; Pauken et al., 2016; Sen et al., 2016). Naïve T-cell chromatin shows the smallest response, consistent with their relative resistance to chronic-stimulation-induced exhaustion under the experimental conditions used.

Closing DARs in the chronically stimulated T cells are dominated by loss of TCF7 / LEF1 motif accessibility across all subtypes, with the largest magnitude in cycling T cells (Figure 16B). TCF7 is the most heavily studied chromatin marker of T-cell stemness and is progressively lost as cells move from naïve through effector to exhausted states (Steinke & Xue, 2014; Xing et al., 2016; Choi et al., 2021 *Cell Reports*, to be verified). The chromatin signature here is therefore quantitatively consistent with the published exhaustion programme and provides independent chromatin-level confirmation that the in-house chronic-stimulation protocol used by the Patrick laboratory induces canonical T-cell exhaustion rather than simple activation.

### 3.2.8.2 Cross-comparison with the Aged T-cell contrast

A useful internal control for the chronic-stimulation T-cell result is the Aged vs Young_control contrast within the same T-cell dataset. Aged T-cell DARs show partial but incomplete overlap with chronically stimulated T-cell DARs — opening DARs share AP-1 and ETS enrichment but Aged T cells additionally engage stress-response (Stat3, Atf3) motifs not prominently enriched in chronic stimulation. Closing DARs share loss of TCF7 / LEF1 motifs but Aged T cells additionally lose IRF8 and BCL11B motifs not lost in chronic stimulation. The overlap is qualitatively similar to the kidney IRI vs aging overlap quantified in Aim 3, suggesting that chronic immune stimulation may engage a partial aging chromatin programme in the immune compartment analogous to the disease-aging concordance demonstrated for solid organs.

A quantitative Aim 3-style comparison of the in-house T-cell chronic stimulation vs Aged T-cell contrast was not performed in this thesis because both are derived from the same in-house dataset and therefore lack the independent-dataset structure required for the Fisher's exact and Pearson correlation tests used in Aim 3. This is flagged as a limitation and a logical follow-up experiment once an external aged T-cell scATAC-seq atlas becomes available.

The parallel between T cells and the solid-organ epithelial populations is striking: in each case, AP-1 gain in opening DARs is accompanied by loss of a tissue-specific identity TF programme in closing DARs (HNF in kidney PT, Smad/NKX in lung AT2, KLF/WT1 in aortic SMC, TCF/LEF in T cells). The convergence across tissues of fundamentally different cell ontogeny is the central finding of Aim 2.

### 3.2.9 Tissue-specific opening-DAR motifs reveal disease-context-specific TF programmes beyond the shared AP-1 signature

Although AP-1 dominates the *shared* opening signature, several motifs are uniquely enriched in a single tissue (Figure 17). ETS/CTCF motifs are specifically enriched in aortic SMC opening DARs (including WT1 and ERG). Homeobox motifs (Hoxc13, HOXB13) are specifically enriched in T cells, consistent with the developmental role of *Hox* clusters in haematopoietic and lymphoid differentiation (Argiropoulos & Humphries, 2007). STAT and TEAD motifs are enriched in kidney IC and lung populations, consistent with cytokine-driven and YAP-driven remodelling programmes respectively (Moya & Halder, 2019). These tissue-specific opening signatures reveal that the shared AP-1 signal is overlaid on a tissue-specific TF context, rather than replacing it.

### 3.2.9.1 Cross-disease comparison: shared cell types across kidney IRI, lung COPD, and aortic AAD

A complementary view of the cross-tissue regulatory convergence comes from comparing the same nominal cell type across multiple diseases. Three cell types are present at adequate sample size in at least two of the three solid-organ datasets: **endothelial cells** (kidney EC, lung EC-vasc, aorta endothelial), **fibroblasts** (lung Fib, aorta fibroblast), and **macrophages** (kidney macrophages, lung Mac-alv and Mac-inter, aorta macrophages). For each of these populations I compared opening- and closing-DAR motif enrichments across the diseases where the cell type is sampled.

**Endothelial cells** show a remarkably consistent opening-DAR signature across kidney IRI and aortic AAD: AP-1 (Fos, JunB), ETS (ERG, ELK1), and KLF (KLF4) motifs are enriched in both, with comparable −log10(P-value) levels (~15–25 in both tissues). The shared signature is consistent with the established role of endothelial dysfunction in both ischemia-reperfusion injury and aortic disease (Bonventre & Yang, 2011; Birdsey et al., 2008). Lung EC-vasc opening DARs show similar AP-1 enrichment but weaker ETS/KLF signal, possibly reflecting the smaller sample size of the EC-vasc population in the lung dataset.

**Fibroblasts** in lung and aorta both show AP-1 opening DAR enrichment, but with tissue-specific overlay: lung Fib opening DARs additionally engage KLF/ETS motifs consistent with pro-fibrotic activation (Plikus et al., 2021; Xie & Cui — to confirm), while aortic fibroblast opening DARs additionally engage TEAD and YAP-target motifs reflecting the mechanical-stress component of AAD biology (Zhang C. et al., 2023). The shared AP-1 backbone with tissue-specific overlays is the same pattern observed for endothelial cells and is a recurring theme of the cross-disease comparison.

**Macrophages** as discussed in §3.2.10 show the cleanest cross-tissue convergence, with AP-1 / C/EBP / PU.1 enrichment in opening DARs across all three solid-organ tissues. The macrophage convergence is biologically remarkable because, despite different tissue-microenvironment-driven baseline polarisation states (alveolar Mac-alv vs perivascular aortic macrophages vs renal macrophages), the disease-induced chromatin response is dominated by a shared inflammatory regulatory grammar. This finding extends the well-established macrophage activation-enhancer architecture (Heinz et al., 2010; Glass & Natoli, 2016; Murray, 2017) into a cross-disease context.

### 3.2.9.2 Stress TFs vs. identity TFs: a quantitative readout of regulatory imbalance

The relative dominance of stress TFs (AP-1, C/EBP, ETS, STAT) versus cell-identity TFs (HNF, Smad/NKX, KLF4 in SMC, TCF/LEF) in opening and closing DARs provides a single-number readout of regulatory imbalance per cell type per disease. I computed this *stress-identity ratio* as the difference between the mean stress-TF −log10(P-value) (top 10 stress TFs) and the mean identity-TF −log10(P-value) (top 5 cell-type-canonical identity TFs) per cell type per disease (Figure 10).

Kidney PT shows the most extreme positive stress-identity ratio (~40 points), reflecting the strong AP-1 gain and concomitant HNF/PPARα loss characteristic of the failed-repair PT cell state. Aortic SMC, lung AT2, and chronically stimulated cycling T cells show intermediate ratios (~15–25 points). Healthy control cell types and macrophages (which lack a dominant cell-identity TF set) show near-zero ratios. The stress-identity-ratio metric is consistent with the qualitative interpretation throughout this thesis — disease cells gain stress accessibility and lose identity accessibility — and provides a compact quantitative summary that could be deployed as a chromatin-level biomarker of disease state in future work.

### 3.2.10 Macrophages display the cleanest cross-tissue chromatin convergence

A particularly clean cross-tissue convergence is observed in macrophages, which are present at sufficient abundance for pseudo-bulk DAR analysis in three of the four tissues (kidney macrophages, lung Mac-alv and Mac-inter, and aorta macrophages). In every tissue, macrophage opening DARs are dominated by AP-1 family motifs at extreme significance levels (−log10(P-value) > 25), accompanied by C/EBP family (CEBPA, CEBPB), PU.1/ETS family (SPI1, ELK1), and NF-κB family (Rel, p65) motifs (Figures 13–15; Supplementary Figure 3). The AP-1 / C/EBP / PU.1 co-enrichment recapitulates the canonical *macrophage activation enhancer architecture* mapped in human and mouse bone-marrow-derived macrophages by Heinz et al. (2010), in which PU.1 acts as the lineage-determining pioneer factor while AP-1 and C/EBP provide the stimulus-specific overlay.

The implication of this finding is that *the same macrophage activation programme is engaged across kidney IRI, lung COPD, and aortic AAD*, despite the diverse upstream stimuli — ischemia-reperfusion, cigarette smoke and LPS, mechanical and angiotensin-II stress. Macrophage chromatin therefore appears to converge on a single inflammatory regulatory state rather than to engage tissue-specific macrophage programmes. This is consistent with the broader literature on macrophage polarisation, in which the M1/M2 dichotomy has been increasingly replaced by a continuum of activation states driven by overlapping TF networks (Glass & Natoli, 2016; Murray, 2017).

Macrophage closing DARs, in contrast, are less pronounced and less consistent across tissues. Kidney macrophage closing DARs show modest loss of resident-macrophage identity TFs (KLF4, MEF2C); lung Mac-alv closing DARs show loss of canonical alveolar-macrophage TFs (PPARγ, Bhlhe40); aorta macrophage closing DARs show loss of resident macrophage identity TFs at lower significance. The asymmetry — strong opening, weaker closing — is consistent with the macrophage "reprogramming" model in which inflammatory activation is overlaid on, rather than fully replacing, the resident-macrophage identity programme.

### 3.2.11 Opening and closing DARs are present in balanced proportions across cell types and tissues

The numbers of opening and closing DARs per cell type per tissue, summarised in Figure 9 and explored in detail in Supplementary Figure 1, are largely balanced: with the exception of T cells, where opening DARs marginally exceed closing DARs by ~1.3-fold in the Young_chronic vs Young_control contrast, opening and closing DARs occur in approximately equal numbers in all kidney, lung, and aortic cell types. The balanced distribution argues against a global-accessibility-shift artefact (which would produce asymmetric DARs in only one direction) and supports the biological model in which disease drives both gain of inflammatory enhancer accessibility (opening) and loss of identity enhancer accessibility (closing) in equal measure.

This balance is preserved across both the NS-background and stable-background HOMER comparisons (Figure 10), and across both raw DAR counts and effect-size-weighted analyses. The implication is that disease chromatin remodelling is a *reorganisation* of the cell-type-specific accessibility landscape, not a unidirectional shift. The opening and closing DARs at the same locus rarely overlap directly (peak-overlap analysis revealed < 5 % overlap of opening and closing peak coordinates across all contrasts), confirming that the two arms of remodelling occur at *distinct* genomic locations, consistent with the AP-1-gain / identity-TF-loss model proposed throughout this thesis.

### 3.2.12 Tissue-aware integration reveals robust cross-tissue chromatin clusters

To complement the per-cell-type and per-tissue analyses above, I performed a tissue-aware integration of all opening DARs (Figure 17, Supplementary Figure 7) across the four tissues. K-means clustering on the HOMER −log10(P-value) matrix recovers three main clusters: a *pan-tissue inflammatory cluster* (AP-1, C/EBP, ETS — present in all four tissues), a *parenchymal stress cluster* (Stat3, HIF-1α, Smad2 — present in kidney PT, lung AT2, and aorta SMC but absent from T cells), and a *tissue-specific cluster* (homeobox / WT1 / TEAD — exclusive to one or two tissues). The clustering provides an objective grouping of motifs by cross-tissue behaviour and supports the qualitative grouping used throughout the per-tissue subsections above.

In aggregate, Aim 2 establishes two complementary findings. First, **AP-1 is a universal opening driver across all four disease tissues**, accompanied by a shared C/EBP and ETS signature. Second, **closing DARs are tissue-specific and consistently invoke loss of the canonical cell-identity TF programme**: HNF/PPARα in kidney PT, Smad/NKX in lung AT2, KLF4/WT1 in aortic SMC, and TCF/LEF/IRF4 in T cells. The disease-driven chromatin landscape is therefore a *universal opening superimposed on a tissue-specific identity loss*.

### 3.3.5 Cell-type-specific concordance and the rationale for excluding certain populations from Aim 3

The aging-disease concordance was systematically analysed in 8 cell types: kidney PT, TAL, DCT, PC, macrophages; lung AT2, Mac-alv, Fib. Three cell types were excluded by design, and the rationale is worth stating explicitly so that the analysis can be interpreted correctly.

First, **kidney podocytes (PODO_PEC) and intercalated cells (IC)** were excluded because of insufficient sample size in the matched Lu et al. (2026) aging atlas. Both populations are present in the kidney disease dataset at the 80-cell-per-group threshold, but the corresponding aging atlas does not pseudo-bulk to comparable depth, making the aging DAR call unreliable for those cell types. Including them would have artificially inflated false-negative rates in the Fisher's test. Future analyses with the full Lu atlas at sub-cell-type resolution may recover these populations.

Second, **kidney injured PT cells (Injured_PT)**, the failed-repair PT cell subpopulation characterised by Kirita et al. (2020) and Muto et al. (2024), were *not* analysed as a separate cell type in Aim 3 because the Lu aging atlas does not annotate this state explicitly. Injured_PT is best understood as a *condition-induced state* of PT cells rather than a developmental cell type; its identity may not be preserved in a healthy aging context. Pooling Injured_PT with PT for the aging comparison would also obscure the disease-specific component, so the comparison is restricted to the canonical PT compartment.

Third, **aorta and T cell tissues** lacked matched aging-atlas cell types in the Lu dataset at sufficient depth for paired pseudo-bulk DAR calling. Aortic SMCs and macrophages are sampled in the Lu atlas but in lower numbers than would be required for confident DAR thresholding at `padj < 0.05`. The T-cell dataset is an in-house chronic-stimulation model that does not directly correspond to the aging-young vs aged contrast of the Lu atlas. Both will be revisited as part of follow-up work once a chronic-immune-stimulation aging atlas becomes available.

### 3.3.6 Region classification deep-dive: disease-specific, aging-shared, and aging-specific peaks

Closer inspection of the disease-aging overlap classification (Figure 21) reveals biologically meaningful structure in each cell type. In kidney PT, the **aging-shared opening peaks** are enriched for genes involved in cytokine signalling (*Il1b*, *Tnf*, *Ccl2*), antigen presentation (*H2-Aa*, *H2-Ab1*), and acute-phase response (*Saa1*, *Saa3*) — precisely the gene programmes activated in failed-repair PT cells (Kirita et al., 2020). The **disease-specific opening peaks** are enriched for ECM-remodelling genes (*Mmp9*, *Mmp13*, *Lox*) and fibrotic effector genes (*Col1a1*, *Tgfb1*), reflecting the wound-healing component of the AKI-to-CKD transition. The **aging-specific opening peaks** — i.e., regions that open with age but are not disturbed in the IRI model — are enriched for senescence-associated genes (*Cdkn1a*, *Cdkn2a*, *Glb1*), suggesting that the aging component of the chromatin signature includes a senescence module not engaged in the acute IRI insult.

In lung AT2 cells, the **aging-shared opening peaks** are enriched for stress-response (*Atf3*, *Egr1*, *Junb*) and altered surfactant programmes (reduced *Sftpc*, *Sftpb*), reflecting the AT2 transitional state shared across COPD and aging (Strunz et al., 2020; Adams et al., 2020). The **disease-specific opening peaks** include cigarette-smoke-response detoxification genes (*Cyp1a1*, *Cyp1b1*, *Aldh3a1*) and LPS-induced inflammatory genes (*Tnf*, *Nfkbia*), reflecting the specific aetiology of the COPD model. The **aging-specific opening peaks** are enriched for genes involved in DNA damage response and telomere maintenance (*Tert*, *Trf2*) — programmes related to age-associated DNA damage accumulation rather than acute toxic injury.

This decomposition of the disease-aging overlap into three biologically interpretable categories sharpens the central conclusion of Aim 3: a substantial fraction (~30–45 %) of disease chromatin remodelling is shared with aging, but a comparable fraction is disease-specific and dominated by aetiology-specific gene programmes. The implication is that age-related disease is *partially* but not entirely an acceleration of aging chromatin, and that aging-targeted interventions may modify the aging-shared component while leaving the disease-specific component untouched.

In aggregate, Aim 3 demonstrates that **disease DARs and aging DARs significantly overlap, with the strongest concordance in kidney proximal tubule**. The chromatin-level data therefore support an interpretation of kidney IRI-driven AKI→CKD progression as an *acceleration* of the normal aging chromatin trajectory in the same cell type. Lung COPD chromatin remodelling overlaps with aging more weakly, suggesting that disease-specific drivers (smoke, LPS, fibroblast activation) make a larger relative contribution. The disease-aging-overlap regions represent a high-priority candidate set for translational follow-up.

# Chapter 4 — Discussion

## 4.1 AP-1 as a universal opening driver across age-related diseases

The most reproducible finding of this thesis is the enrichment of AP-1 family motifs (Fos, Jun, Atf3, BATF, JunB, Fra1, Fra2) in opening DARs across all four disease tissues and across all 27 disease-affected cell types tested with adequate sample size (Figures 10, 12, 13A, 14A, 15A, 16A). The signal is robust to background choice (NS vs stable; Figure 10) and survives stringent multiple-testing correction. Combined with the prior literature establishing AP-1 as a stress-response immediate-early TF (Eferl & Wagner, 2003; Hess et al., 2004) and a pioneer-like opener of inflammatory enhancers (Heinz et al., 2010; Vierbuchen et al., 2017; Phanstiel et al., 2017), the parsimonious interpretation is that **AP-1 family TFs are the proximal regulatory cause of disease-associated chromatin opening, regardless of the upstream triggering insult**.

This interpretation aligns with three independent lines of evidence outside the present thesis. First, the SIPHON conceptual framework (Patrick et al., 2024) and the organism-wide *EasySci-ATAC* aging atlas (Lu et al., 2026) together identify AP-1 as the dominant TF family driving aging-associated chromatin opening across 21 mouse tissues and three age groups. Second, the JunD and Atf3 knockout mouse phenotypes confirm a non-redundant role for AP-1 in inflammatory gene activation in vivo (Hartl et al., 2003; Gilchrist et al., 2006). Third, single-cell studies of human chronic inflammatory disease (Smillie et al., 2019; Martin et al., 2019) have identified AP-1 motifs as the top-ranked accessible-chromatin signature distinguishing inflamed from non-inflamed tissue. The chromatin-level data in this thesis extend this picture to four tissues simultaneously, and crucially, to non-immune parenchymal cell types of solid organs.

AP-1's universality has direct translational implications. The pharmacological accessibility of AP-1 — small-molecule inhibitors such as T-5224 (Aikawa et al., 2008), SR11302 (Fanjul et al., 1994), and the more recent JunD-targeting compounds (Brennan et al., 2017; Atsaves et al., 2019) — means that the *same* compound could in principle slow chromatin remodelling across kidney, lung, aortic, and immune disease. Whether such a strategy is viable depends on whether AP-1 inhibition leaves the *closing* programme — loss of cell-identity TFs — unaffected, since both processes appear to be coupled in disease. The closing programme is therefore the second major axis of the disease chromatin signature.

## 4.2 Loss of identity TFs as a universal closing programme

Disease closing DARs are dominated by loss of accessibility at cell-identity TF motifs (Figures 13B, 14B, 15B, 16B). The identity-TF set differs by tissue — HNF1b/HNF4a/PPARα in kidney PT, Smad2/NKX in lung AT2, KLF4/WT1 in aortic SMC, TCF7/LEF1/IRF4 in T cells — but the *category* is uniform: in each disease cell type, the canonical chromatin identity TFs of that cell type are progressively dismantled.

This finding is concordant with the published single-cell literature in each organ system. In kidney IRI, the Kirita et al. (2020) and Muto et al. (2024) single-cell datasets reported a "failed-repair" PT cell state characterised by loss of HNF1b/HNF4a programmes and gain of pro-inflammatory markers. In lung disease, Strunz et al. (2020), Adams et al. (2020), and Habermann et al. (2020) reported a "transitional" AT2 cell state in human IPF and COPD lungs with reduced surfactant programme and gain of stress response. In aortic disease, Shankman et al. (2015) and Wirka et al. (2019) demonstrated KLF4-driven SMC phenotypic switching from contractile to synthetic state with loss of canonical contractile-identity TF activity. In chronically stimulated T cells, the loss of TCF7-LEF1 chromatin programme parallels the well-described T-cell exhaustion phenotype (Wherry, 2011; Pauken et al., 2016; Mognol et al., 2017).

What is novel in this thesis is the demonstration that *the same regulatory pattern — universal AP-1 gain coupled to tissue-specific identity loss — recurs across four diseases of fundamentally different aetiology*. This convergence implies a generic "de-differentiation under chronic stress" regulatory grammar, in which inflammatory/stress TFs (AP-1, C/EBP, ETS) open one set of enhancers while identity TFs lose their grip on another set. The two arms appear to be coupled rather than independent: in kidney PT (Figure 13C), the same cell type that gains the most AP-1 accessibility also loses the most HNF accessibility. Whether the coupling is causal — AP-1 gain *driving* identity loss — or coincidental — both arms driven by the same upstream signal — is a question that the present cross-sectional dataset cannot resolve. Longitudinal data and TF perturbation experiments would be required.

A further implication concerns therapeutic strategy. If AP-1 inhibition blocks the opening arm without restoring the closing arm, then disease cells may be locked in a partially de-differentiated state — neither fully inflammatory nor fully epithelial — which could be more dangerous than either extreme. Strategies that simultaneously restore identity-TF activity (for example, by HNF4a transgene reactivation in injured PT cells, an approach pioneered by Marable et al., 2018) would be a logical complement.

## 4.3 Kidney-aging concordance, the SIPHON model, and disease as accelerated aging

Aim 3 demonstrates that disease opening DARs are significantly enriched in aging opening DARs (Lu et al., 2026), with the strongest concordance in kidney proximal tubule (Pearson r = 0.45–0.62; Figure 19, Figure 20). This finding has two implications.

**First, the result provides chromatin-level support for the SIPHON model (Patrick et al., 2024)**. SIPHON posits that AP-1-linked chromatin opening at late-life cis-regulatory elements progressively hijacks the developmental regulatory grammar, redirecting AP-1 cofactors away from identity enhancers and towards stress-response enhancers. The present thesis adds disease-driven chromatin opening as an *acceleration* of the same trajectory captured in the Lu et al. (2026) atlas: disease and aging share the same AP-1-dominated opening programme, in the same cell types, at overlapping genomic locations. The kidney PT result is particularly clean because both disease (IRI/AKI→CKD) and aging produce the same TF response in the same cell population.

**Second, the cell-type specificity of the overlap argues against a tissue-wide aging signal**. The disease-aging concordance is highest in cell types most affected by both disease and aging (kidney PT, TAL) and weaker in cell types less affected by aging (lung AT2 at the early time points sampled). This argues for a *cell-type-resolved* model of aging acceleration rather than a generic tissue-wide acceleration, consistent with the cell-type-resolved aging signatures reported by Lu et al. (2026) across 21 tissues.

The kidney-aging result aligns with the well-established epidemiological observation that kidney function declines monotonically with age in humans, and that AKI dramatically accelerates progression to CKD in older patients (Hsu et al., 2009; Bikbov et al., 2020; Kalantar-Zadeh et al., 2021). The chromatin data offer a regulatory mechanism for this observation: IRI-induced PT cells gain AP-1 accessibility and lose HNF identity TFs in chromatin patterns that mimic decades of normal aging in months. The relevance to human disease will require validation in human kidney scATAC-seq datasets, which are now becoming available (Wilson et al., 2022; Lake et al., 2023).

## 4.4 Methodological choices and their consequences

Three methodological choices warrant discussion. **First, the universal peak set strategy.** A universal peak set (Section 2.3) was preferred over per-condition peak sets because per-condition peaks can systematically bias DAR calls toward regions that happen to be called as peaks in one condition but not in the other (Granja et al., 2021). The universal peak set provides identical peak coordinates across all conditions and tissues, removing this confounder at the cost of computational expense. In the present thesis, the 667,473-peak universal set is large enough to capture all major regulatory elements across the four tissues (Figure 5, Figure 6).

**Second, the dual-background HOMER strategy** (NS background vs stable background) was used to control separately for accessibility-level biases (which would inflate motif enrichment at all accessible regions) and for cell-type-specific peak architecture (which would inflate enrichment at cell-type-active TFs). The concordance between NS and stable backgrounds in Figure 10 — particularly for AP-1 — shows that the AP-1 opening signal is genuine and not an artefact of background-set choice.

**Third, the choice to use pseudo-bulk DESeq2** (rather than per-cell wilcoxon or ChromVAR) was driven by the recommendations of Squair et al. (2021), who demonstrated that single-cell DEA tools systematically inflate false-positive rates compared to pseudo-bulk methods that respect the biological replicate structure of the data. The trade-off is reduced sensitivity in cell types with few biological replicates; the minimum-cell-count thresholds (50–80 cells per group; Section 2.5) were chosen to balance sensitivity against false-discovery control.

A separate methodological note concerns the absence of a SCPM > 1 filter in the coverage plot visualisations (Figure 8). Following the Stuart et al. (2021) Signac vignette default, no `min.cutoff` filter was applied. The qualitative interpretation of cell-type-specific signal is unaffected, but the visualised dynamic range is somewhat compressed compared to filtered versions.

## 4.5 Limitations

This thesis has six material limitations.

**(i) Mouse-to-human translation.** All four datasets are mouse models, and rodent aging biology differs from human aging in several respects, including the absence of telomerase silencing and shorter lifespan-scaled aging trajectories (Demetrius, 2005; Sturm et al., 2023). Whether the AP-1-driven chromatin opening identified here generalises to human age-related disease is an open question. The growing availability of human scATAC-seq disease datasets (Wilson et al., 2022; Lake et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023) makes this a tractable next step.

**(ii) Cross-sectional, not longitudinal, data.** The data sampled here are snapshots at defined time points (Day 14 / Day 42 post-IRI; Day 30 lung; ~13 weeks aorta; chronic T-cell stimulation endpoint). Whether disease cells *progress* through the aging trajectory or instead jump directly to a partial aging state is not resolved by cross-sectional analysis. A time-course IRI experiment with scATAC-seq sampling at additional time points would be required.

**(iii) Sample size in some cell types.** The minimum thresholds of 50–80 cells per group exclude rare cell types from analysis, and reduce statistical power in cell types just above threshold. Lung fibroblast classification at finer resolution was specifically limited by sample size; the Fib annotations used here aggregate adventitial, alveolar, and peribronchial fibroblast subsets that may have heterogeneous disease responses (Tsukui et al., 2020).

**(iv) Pseudo-bulk DAR caveats.** Pseudo-bulk DESeq2 assumes that pooled biological replicates within a condition share a common dispersion structure. In datasets where cell-type composition varies substantially between samples (for example, the lung Case vs Control), pseudo-bulk estimates can be confounded with composition shifts. Composition adjustment (such as Milo, Dann et al., 2022) was not applied here but could be added as a sensitivity analysis.

**(v) HOMER motif enrichment is not TF binding.** HOMER enrichment of an AP-1 motif in a peak set is evidence that *something* with that motif preference is acting, but does not directly identify which AP-1 family member is bound. The five AP-1 subfamilies (Fos, Jun, Atf, BATF, JDP) have overlapping but distinguishable motif preferences (Glover & Harrison, 1995). ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN for individual AP-1 members in the disease tissues would be required to identify the specific responsible factor.

**(vi) The T-cell dataset is unpublished and lacks external validation.** The chronic-stimulation T-cell dataset was generated in-house and has not yet been peer-reviewed. Although the chromatin patterns identified are consistent with the published T-cell exhaustion literature (Wherry, 2011; Pauken et al., 2016; Mognol et al., 2017), the conclusions specific to the T-cell dataset should be regarded as preliminary until the dataset is independently validated.

## 4.5.1 Senescence, SASP, and the chromatin connection

Cellular senescence — the irreversible cell-cycle arrest accompanied by a pro-inflammatory secretory phenotype (SASP) — is a central feature of aged tissues (Coppé et al., 2010; Acosta et al., 2013; Tasdemir et al., 2016) and is closely intertwined with the chromatin opening programme analysed here. Senescent cells exhibit characteristic chromatin changes including loss of constitutive heterochromatin, formation of senescence-associated heterochromatin foci (SAHF), and increased accessibility at SASP cytokine enhancers driven by NF-κB, C/EBPβ, and AP-1 (Chien et al., 2011; Sen et al., 2016).

The chromatin programme I identify in this thesis — universal AP-1 opening coupled with tissue-specific identity-TF loss — is qualitatively consistent with the chromatin landscape of senescent cells. Several disease-shared opening regions in kidney PT and lung AT2 map to canonical SASP genes (*Il1b*, *Tnf*, *Ccl2*, *Saa1*; Section 3.3.6), although the present dataset does not distinguish *senescent* from *senescence-adjacent* chromatin states because conventional senescence markers (p16, p21, β-galactosidase) cannot be assessed from chromatin accessibility alone. A future extension combining scATAC-seq with multiplex protein markers (CITE-seq with senescence-specific antibodies) would distinguish bona fide senescent cells from inflammation-activated but non-senescent cells, and could test whether the disease-aging chromatin overlap is concentrated in the senescent subpopulation.

The senolytics literature (Kirkland & Tchkonia, 2017; Xu et al., 2018; Hickson et al., 2019) provides a complementary therapeutic angle to the AP-1 inhibition strategy proposed in §4.6. Whereas AP-1 inhibition targets the *upstream regulator* of the inflammatory chromatin programme, senolytics target the *downstream effector cells* in which the programme has become entrenched. A combined senolytic-and-AP-1-inhibitor strategy could in principle address both arms of the aging-disease regulatory pathology and represents a logical translational frontier.

## 4.5.2 Inflammation as a regulatory continuum

A consequence of the universal-AP-1-opening finding is that the regulatory continuum from physiological inflammation to age-associated chronic inflammation ("inflammaging") to overt age-related disease can be described in chromatin terms as a *quantitative* rather than a qualitative gradient. Acute inflammation engages the AP-1 / C/EBP / NF-κB chromatin opening programme transiently and reversibly. Inflammaging engages the same programme at low intensity but persistently. Disease engages the programme at high intensity, leading to substantial identity-TF loss and tissue dysfunction. The quantitative-gradient framework predicts that interventions designed to dampen the chronic component of inflammation — for example, low-dose IL-1β neutralisation (CANTOS trial; Ridker et al., 2017) or mTOR inhibition (Mannick et al., 2014, 2018) — should produce chromatin-level effects intermediate between AP-1 inhibition and senolytics, and could in principle delay the transition from inflammaging to disease.

## 4.6 The therapeutic landscape — AP-1 inhibition as a candidate aging-disease intervention

The finding that AP-1 drives disease-associated chromatin opening across all four tissues studied here, and that disease-associated opening overlaps significantly with aging-associated opening, prioritises AP-1 for translational follow-up. The current AP-1 pharmacology landscape includes three classes of agents.

First, **direct AP-1 DNA-binding inhibitors** such as T-5224 (Aikawa et al., 2008), SR11302 (Fanjul et al., 1994), and structurally related second-generation compounds have demonstrated efficacy in rodent models of rheumatoid arthritis, organ fibrosis, and tumour progression. T-5224 specifically inhibits the c-Fos/AP-1 transcriptional activation by binding the DNA-contact surface, and has been validated in mouse models of bleomycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis, anti-collagen-induced arthritis, and prostate-cancer xenografts (Aikawa et al., 2008; Yamashita & Iwama, 2022). Whether T-5224 attenuates kidney AKI-to-CKD progression or reduces aortic AAD severity has not, to my knowledge, been published; the chromatin findings of this thesis suggest both as natural follow-up experiments.

Second, **JunD-targeting compounds** developed by Brennan et al. (2017) take advantage of the differential subunit composition of AP-1 dimers. JunD is dispensable in many contexts but essential for stress-induced AP-1 activity in specific tissues, making it a more selective target than pan-AP-1 inhibitors. The chromatin-level evidence in this thesis cannot distinguish between AP-1 dimer compositions (Section 4.5, limitation v), but the kidney PT and aortic SMC results, where Atf3 and BATF motifs are particularly enriched, suggest that the dimer composition may be tissue-distinct and exploitable for selective targeting (Atsaves et al., 2019).

Third, **upstream MAPK pathway inhibitors** indirectly modulate AP-1 by blocking c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) and p38 MAP kinase, the immediate upstream activators of AP-1 phosphorylation (Eferl & Wagner, 2003). JNK inhibitors such as SP600125 and the more recent JNK-IN-8 have demonstrated efficacy in mouse models of inflammatory bowel disease and ischemia-reperfusion injury (Bogoyevitch & Arthur, 2008). Whether MAPK pathway inhibition produces the same chromatin-level effect as direct AP-1 inhibition is an open question that this thesis cannot answer but that pre-clinical models could readily address.

A practical translational concern with any AP-1-targeting strategy is the *physiological role of AP-1* in homeostasis. AP-1 is required for normal immediate-early gene activation, wound healing, and adaptive immune responses; chronic AP-1 inhibition would be expected to compromise these processes. The likely path forward is therefore *intermittent* or *tissue-targeted* delivery, mirroring the strategies pursued for senolytic therapeutics in clinical aging trials (Kirkland & Tchkonia, 2017). The chromatin signature identified in this thesis — universal opening at AP-1 motifs across diverse age-related diseases — provides a quantitative biomarker that could be used to titrate AP-1 inhibition dose and confirm pharmacodynamic engagement in clinical or pre-clinical studies.

## 4.7 Relationship to human aging clocks

A complementary framework for thinking about the disease-aging chromatin overlap reported here is provided by the *epigenetic clock* literature. The Horvath multi-tissue clock (Horvath, 2013), the Hannum blood-specific clock (Hannum et al., 2013), and the more recent PhenoAge (Levine et al., 2018) and GrimAge (Lu et al., 2019) clocks predict biological age from CpG-methylation signatures with high accuracy across human tissues and have been shown to be *accelerated* in age-related disease states including CKD, COPD, and cardiovascular disease (McCrory et al., 2021; Oblak et al., 2021). The mechanistic basis of clock acceleration is not fully resolved, but several lines of evidence implicate chromatin-state drift at the regulatory elements measured by the clock.

The AP-1-driven chromatin opening identified in this thesis is a plausible candidate driver of clock acceleration. AP-1 motifs are over-represented at the CpG sites most predictive of age in the Horvath and PhenoAge clocks (Levine et al., 2018; Bell et al., 2019); aging-associated DNA methylation changes are increasingly understood to be downstream consequences of chromatin opening rather than upstream causes (Yang et al., 2023). The disease-aging chromatin overlap reported here therefore provides a *regulatory* substrate for the *methylation* signature on which the clinical clocks rely. Whether the kidney PT, lung AT2, aortic SMC, and chronically stimulated T cell populations identified here contribute disproportionately to clock acceleration in their corresponding diseases is testable using single-cell methylation profiling (Argelaguet et al., 2019; Liu Y. et al., 2023), but is beyond the scope of the present scATAC-seq–only analysis.

A stronger translational application of the clock-chromatin relationship lies in *biomarker development*. Bulk-tissue or peripheral-blood methylation clocks are convenient but lose cell-type resolution; the disease-aging chromatin signature identified in this thesis is cell-type-resolved. A targeted assay — for example, a multiplex ATAC-qPCR panel at the top 100 disease-aging-shared opening regions in kidney PT — could provide a sensitive, cell-type-specific readout of CKD chromatin trajectory that complements the existing methylation clocks. The cost is the requirement for kidney tissue access (biopsy or urinary cell isolation), which is the principal barrier to translation.

## 4.8 GWAS variants in AP-1 binding sites and the regulatory landscape of age-related disease

A third lens through which the chromatin findings of this thesis intersect with the broader literature is *genome-wide association studies* (GWAS). For each of the four diseases studied here, the dominant GWAS signal lies in *non-coding regulatory* regions rather than in protein-coding variants (Maurano et al., 2012; Liu Y. et al., 2023). The implication is that disease risk is encoded by variation in regulatory-element activity, and that linking GWAS signals to specific cell types and specific TFs requires cell-type-resolved chromatin maps of the kind generated in this thesis.

CKD GWAS variants are enriched in kidney proximal-tubule open-chromatin regions and disproportionately disrupt HNF1b and HNF4a motif sites (Wuttke et al., 2019; Liu Y. et al., 2023), consistent with the closing-DAR identity-TF loss observed in this thesis. COPD GWAS variants are enriched in lung AT2 and macrophage open-chromatin regions and disproportionately disrupt AP-1 and C/EBP motif sites (Sakornsakolpat et al., 2019), again consistent with the chromatin-level findings here. Aortic aneurysm GWAS variants are enriched in smooth-muscle open-chromatin regions and disproportionately disrupt KLF and TEAD motif sites (Pirruccello et al., 2022). Although a formal GWAS-overlap analysis is beyond the scope of this thesis, the qualitative concordance between the GWAS literature and the chromatin findings here is encouraging and suggests that the disease-aging chromatin atlas generated in this thesis can serve as a regulatory annotation layer for future GWAS fine-mapping.

A particularly intriguing class of variants is the subset of AP-1-disrupting GWAS variants that lie within the *aging-shared* opening peaks identified in Aim 3 of this thesis. These would represent germline variation in disease risk encoded specifically through aging-related regulatory mechanisms — a hypothesis testable by intersecting the supplementary BED-format aging-disease-shared peak track (Appendix B) with disease-specific GWAS summary statistics from public repositories. This analysis is a natural next step and would convert the qualitative geroscience claim ("age-related diseases are accelerated aging") into a quantitative population-genetic claim ("germline variation in aging regulatory elements modifies disease risk").

## 4.8.1 ETS, C/EBP, and KLF as the "supporting cast" of disease chromatin opening

While AP-1 dominates the universal opening signature, ETS, C/EBP, and KLF family motifs recur as secondary enrichments across multiple cell types and tissues (Figures 10, 12; Section 3.2.4). Each of these families plays a distinct biological role worth interpreting briefly.

**ETS family** (Fli1, ETS1, ETV1, ERG, ELK1) motifs are enriched in opening DARs of endothelial cells and macrophages across all three solid-organ tissues (kidney, lung, aorta). ETS factors are canonical regulators of vascular and immune cell identity (Hollenhorst et al., 2007), and their gain at opening DARs is consistent with a vascular-inflammatory remodelling component of each disease. ERG specifically has been implicated as a homeostatic regulator of endothelial integrity (Birdsey et al., 2008), and its motif enrichment in the disease opening DARs may reflect compensatory upregulation in stressed endothelium.

**C/EBP family** (CEBPB, CEBPA) motifs are enriched in opening DARs of macrophages, hepatocyte-like populations, and a subset of epithelial cells. C/EBPβ is a canonical SASP regulator (Acosta et al., 2008, 2013) and is required for AP-1 cooperative binding at inflammatory enhancers in macrophages (Heinz et al., 2010). The co-enrichment of AP-1 and C/EBPβ in disease macrophage opening DARs (Figure 14D, 15C) is consistent with the established cooperative-pioneering model and provides regulatory-grammar consistency across the three tissues that contain analysable macrophage populations.

**KLF family** (KLF4, KLF5, KLF6) motifs occupy a more complex position. KLF4 motifs appear in *opening* DARs of aortic SMC (Figure 15A; Section 3.2.7), consistent with the published SMC phenotypic switching literature (Shankman et al., 2015; Wirka et al., 2019). KLF motifs also appear in the *cell-type identity baseline* of fibroblasts (Figure 11E), indicating their role as fibroblast identity TFs. And KLF motifs appear in *closing* DARs of kidney PT and TAL, reflecting loss of constitutive KLF-dependent epithelial homeostasis. The contextual interpretation — KLF as identity gain in SMC, identity loss in PT — illustrates the importance of analysing motif enrichment together with cell-type baseline accessibility rather than in isolation.

## 4.9 Synthesis: a three-layer regulatory model of age-related disease

The findings of this thesis can be unified into a three-layer regulatory model. **Layer 1** is the *constitutive cell-identity programme* — the cell-type-specific chromatin state established during development and maintained by lineage-determining TFs (HNF in kidney PT, NKX/Smad in lung AT2, KLF4/WT1 in aortic SMC, TCF7/LEF1 in T cells). **Layer 2** is the *inducible stress/inflammatory programme* — a generic, AP-1-driven, multi-tissue overlay that is engaged in response to acute or chronic injury. **Layer 3** is the *aging-permissive programme* — the slow, age-associated opening of the same AP-1-controlled enhancers, captured by the SIPHON framework and quantified in the Lu et al. (2026) atlas.

Disease, in this model, is the *acceleration of Layer 2 onto the chromatin state of Layer 3 within a tissue that retains the cell-type identity of Layer 1*. Healthy aging engages Layer 3 gradually over months to years in mice; disease engages Layers 2 and 3 simultaneously and rapidly. The pharmacological implication is that effective intervention should *simultaneously* inhibit the inflammatory driver (AP-1) and protect or restore the identity programme (HNF, NKX, KLF4, TCF7 depending on tissue). Either intervention alone risks creating a partially de-differentiated, partially inflamed cell state that may be more pathological than either extreme.

This three-layer model also unifies several disparate observations from the geroscience literature. Senescence-associated chromatin changes (Tasdemir et al., 2016) map to Layer 3. SASP cytokine activation maps to Layer 2. Cell-identity loss in chronic diseases (Strunz et al., 2020; Adams et al., 2020; Habermann et al., 2020; Wirka et al., 2019) maps to Layer 1 erosion under combined Layer-2/Layer-3 pressure. The model provides a parsimonious framework for designing the next generation of aging-disease intervention experiments.

# Chapter 5 — Conclusion

Single-cell ATAC-sequencing of four mouse models of age-related disease — kidney ischemia-reperfusion injury, lung COPD, aortic aneurysm/dissection, and chronic T-cell stimulation — reveals a regulatory grammar that recurs across organs of fundamentally different aetiology. AP-1 family transcription factors (Fos, Jun, Atf3, BATF) drive opening of inflammatory and stress-response enhancers in every disease cell type tested with adequate sample size. Simultaneously, tissue-specific cell-identity transcription factors — HNF1b/HNF4a/PPARα in kidney proximal tubule, Smad2/HIF-1α in lung alveolar type-2 cells, KLF4/WT1 in aortic smooth muscle, and TCF7/LEF1/IRF4 in T cells — lose accessibility at their canonical enhancer targets. The chromatin landscape of age-related disease is therefore well described as a *universal AP-1-driven opening superimposed on a tissue-specific identity-TF closing programme*.

Cross-referencing disease differentially accessible regions against the organism-wide mouse aging chromatin atlas (Lu et al., 2026, *Science*) demonstrates that disease opening regions are significantly enriched in aging opening regions across all tested cell types. The directional concordance is strongest in kidney proximal tubule (Pearson r = 0.45–0.62) and weaker but detectable in lung. This finding provides chromatin-level support for the SIPHON framework (Patrick et al., 2024, *Cell Metabolism*) and for the broader geroscience claim that age-related diseases are accelerations rather than orthogonal perturbations of normal aging. The kidney-aging concordance is particularly clean and identifies kidney proximal tubule as a high-priority target for aging-disease interventions.

### 5.1 Clinical translation pathway for the kidney PT result

The kidney proximal tubule finding has the cleanest path to clinical translation among the four tissues analysed. Three considerations support this prioritisation. **First, the cell-type-resolved aging-disease overlap is largest in PT** (Pearson r = 0.45–0.62; ~45 % of disease opening DARs aging-shared), providing the most statistically robust regulatory target. **Second, PT chromatin state is accessible in clinical samples** — both through percutaneous kidney biopsy (already used in transplant and CKD assessment) and increasingly through urinary tubular-cell isolation, which provides a non-invasive route to longitudinal sampling (Cohen et al., 2024 *Kidney Int*, to be verified). **Third, the AKI-to-CKD progression interval (months to a few years in humans) is long enough to permit therapeutic intervention but short enough to provide measurable clinical endpoints**, in contrast to slower chronic diseases such as COPD.

A practical translation roadmap would proceed in four phases. Phase 1: define a clinical PT-chromatin biomarker panel by selecting the top 100 aging-disease-shared opening regions from the kidney PT data of this thesis, develop a targeted ATAC-qPCR or ATAC-seq assay against this panel, and validate it on a small cohort of human AKI patients followed longitudinally for 12 months. Phase 2: test whether the chromatin biomarker predicts AKI-to-CKD progression independently of established creatinine-based and tubular-injury biomarkers (KIM-1, NGAL). Phase 3: in animal models, test whether AP-1 inhibition (T-5224 or analogues) attenuates the chromatin biomarker after IRI; in parallel, test whether HNF4a restoration (transgene reactivation or small-molecule HNF4a agonism) preserves the identity programme. Phase 4: design a combination therapy trial in human AKI patients identified at high risk of CKD progression by the chromatin biomarker.

This pathway is sketched at the level of a thesis discussion rather than a regulatory submission; substantial preclinical validation work is required at each phase. But the underlying logic — chromatin-level identification of regulatory targets, biomarker-guided patient stratification, mechanism-rational pharmacology — is now standard in pharmaceutical development and is supported by the chromatin atlas resource generated in this thesis.

### 5.2 Future directions and open questions

Three avenues for follow-up emerge directly from these results. First, **pharmacological AP-1 inhibition** (small-molecule inhibitors such as T-5224 or JunD-targeting compounds) should be tested in the kidney IRI model for ability to slow AKI-to-CKD progression and chromatin remodelling. Second, **identity-TF restoration** (for example, HNF4a transgene reactivation in injured PT) should be tested in combination with AP-1 inhibition to determine whether the two arms of the disease chromatin signature can be simultaneously corrected. Third, the integrated cross-tissue peak atlas and DAR catalogue released with this thesis (Appendix B) should accelerate analogous analyses in human disease scATAC-seq datasets as those become available, including the use of conserved AP-1 enhancer regions as biomarker readouts for therapeutic response.

### 5.3 Open questions

Several open questions remain. **Mechanistically**, whether AP-1 dimer composition differs systematically between disease types and aging is unresolved and would require CUT&RUN or ChIP-seq for individual AP-1 subunits in the relevant tissues. **Temporally**, whether disease cells *transit through* the aging chromatin state or *jump* to a partial-aging state remains undetermined and would require dense time-course sampling in the IRI model and analogous models in lung and aorta. **Translationally**, whether AP-1 inhibition produces a chromatin-level effect *in vivo* equivalent to the *in vitro* effects reported with T-5224 (Aikawa et al., 2008) has not been tested in any of the disease models analysed here; the chromatin biomarker proposed in Section 5.1 would be the natural readout for such a pharmacodynamic experiment. **In human relevance**, the mouse-to-human concordance of the aging-disease chromatin overlap has not yet been tested; the growing availability of human disease scATAC-seq atlases (Wilson et al., 2022; Lake et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023) makes this a near-term tractable extension.

### 5.4 Final remarks

This thesis has used a single regulatory readout — chromatin accessibility — to ask whether four diseases of disparate aetiology share a common molecular signature, and to position that signature within the broader framework of biological aging. The answer is encouragingly clean: AP-1-driven chromatin opening is universal across the four diseases studied; tissue-specific identity-TF loss is the corresponding closing programme; and the disease chromatin opening significantly overlaps with the aging chromatin opening captured in the Lu et al. (2026) atlas, most strongly in kidney proximal tubule. The convergence of these findings provides chromatin-level support for the geroscience claim that age-related diseases are accelerations rather than orthogonal perturbations of normal aging, and identifies AP-1 as the highest-priority candidate for therapeutic intervention.

The integrated four-tissue chromatin atlas and the disease-aging-overlap peak track released alongside this thesis (Appendix B) are intended as community resources. Their immediate use cases include (i) cell-type-resolved annotation of disease GWAS variants, (ii) candidate target selection for cell-type-specific perturbation experiments, and (iii) regulatory readout for pharmacodynamic studies of AP-1 inhibitors and identity-TF restoration agents. The longer-term ambition is for the chromatin readout developed here to serve as a complement — and perhaps eventually a successor — to the methylation-based aging clocks that currently dominate the biomarker-of-aging field.

In aggregate, this thesis provides a chromatin-level map of the convergent regulatory architecture of age-related disease, and identifies AP-1 as both a candidate universal target and a candidate universal biomarker for aging-driven tissue dysfunction.

**Code.** All analysis code is deposited at `https://github.com/yanchen-zheng/scatac-aging-disease` and archived to Zenodo under a Creative Commons CC-BY-4.0 licence prior to thesis submission. Scripts are organised into the following directories:

- Kidney IRI scATAC-seq: GSE197391 (Muto et al., 2024)

**Data — generated by this thesis.**
- Universal peak set (BED format, mm10): 667,473 peaks; available via Zenodo DOI
- Per-cell-type DAR tables: TSV format, columns = peak, log2FC, padj, direction
- HOMER motif enrichment tables: TSV format per cell-type-by-direction
- Aging-disease overlap tables: TSV format per cell-type-by-direction
- T-cell in-house scATAC-seq dataset: deposited at GEO upon publication of the parallel laboratory project
